Islamic Revert Guide

Resources and guidance for new Muslims and those interested in Islam

Welcome to Islam

Congratulations on your journey to Islam! The word "revert" is often used instead of "convert" because in Islam, it's believed that everyone is born in a natural state of submission to God (Islam), and later life circumstances lead people away from this path. So when someone embraces Islam, they're actually "reverting" back to their natural state.

This guide aims to provide essential information, resources, and support for your journey as a new Muslim. Remember that your journey is personal, and it's okay to progress at your own pace.

The Essential Beliefs

Islam is built upon six fundamental beliefs and five practical pillars:

Six Articles of Faith:

Belief in Allah

Belief in the One God, known as Allah in Arabic, who is unique, incomparable, and the only one worthy of worship.

Belief in Angels

Belief in Allah's angels, created from light, who never disobey Allah and carry out His commands.

Belief in Divine Books

Belief in Allah's books, including the Quran, Torah, Psalms, and Gospel in their original form.

Belief in Prophets

Belief in Allah's messengers, including Adam, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad (peace be upon them all).

Belief in the Day of Judgment

Belief in the Day of Judgment, when everyone will be held accountable for their actions.

Belief in Divine Decree

Belief in Allah's divine decree, that nothing happens without His knowledge and permission.

Five Pillars of Islam:

Shahada

The testimony of faith: "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is His messenger."

Learn More

The Shahada (testimony of faith) is the first pillar of Islam and the gateway to becoming Muslim. When you declare this with sincere belief, you become a Muslim. In Arabic, it's pronounced: "Ash-hadu an la ilaha illa Allah, wa ash-hadu anna Muhammadan Rasul Allah."

Salah

The five daily prayers that establish a direct connection with Allah.

Learn More

Muslims pray five times daily: Fajr (dawn), Dhuhr (noon), Asr (afternoon), Maghrib (sunset), and Isha (night). Prayer involves physical movements and recitations, creating a comprehensive connection with Allah. Before praying, Muslims perform wudu (ablution) to purify themselves.

Zakat

Annual charity of 2.5% of one's wealth, purifying wealth and helping those in need.

Learn More

Zakat is an annual obligation for Muslims who possess wealth above a specific threshold (nisab). It's calculated at 2.5% of eligible wealth after one lunar year of ownership. Zakat helps purify wealth and supports the poor, needy, and other specified categories of recipients.

Sawm

Fasting during the month of Ramadan, abstaining from food, drink, and physical desires from dawn to sunset.

Learn More

During Ramadan, the ninth month of the Islamic calendar, Muslims fast from dawn until sunset. Fasting means abstaining from food, drink, smoking, and marital relations during daylight hours. It teaches self-discipline, empathy for the less fortunate, and spiritual awareness. Those who are sick, traveling, elderly, pregnant, nursing, or menstruating are exempted but may make up the days later or give charity instead.

Hajj

Pilgrimage to Mecca, obligatory once in a lifetime for those who are physically and financially able.

Learn More

Hajj is the annual pilgrimage to Mecca, Saudi Arabia, during the Islamic month of Dhul Hijjah. It's obligatory once in a lifetime for Muslims who are physically and financially able. Hajj commemorates the experiences of Prophet Ibrahim and his family, and represents unity, equality, and submission to Allah.

Learning How to Pray

Prayer (Salah) is essential in Islam and performed five times daily. Here's a simplified guide to help you get started:

Steps to Perform Wudu (Ablution)

  1. Make the intention (niyyah) to perform wudu
  2. Say "Bismillah" (In the name of Allah)
  3. Wash hands up to the wrists three times
  4. Rinse mouth and nose three times
  5. Wash face from forehead to chin and ear to ear three times
  6. Wash arms up to the elbows three times (right arm first)
  7. Wipe the top of the head with wet hands once
  8. Wipe over and behind the ears with wet fingers once
  9. Wash feet up to the ankles three times (right foot first)

Basic Prayer Steps

  1. Stand facing the Qibla (direction of the Kaaba in Mecca)
  2. Make the intention for the specific prayer
  3. Raise hands to shoulders or ears saying "Allahu Akbar" (Allah is Greatest)
  4. Place right hand over left on chest
  5. Recite the opening chapter of the Quran (Surah Al-Fatiha) and another short passage
  6. Bow (Ruku) saying "Allahu Akbar"
  7. Rise saying "Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah" (Allah hears those who praise Him)
  8. Prostrate (Sujood) saying "Allahu Akbar"
  9. Sit between prostrations saying "Allahu Akbar"
  10. Prostrate again saying "Allahu Akbar"
  11. Complete the remaining units (rak'ahs) similarly
  12. At the end, recite the tashahhud and salutations to the Prophet
  13. Conclude by turning head right and left saying "Assalamu alaikum wa rahmatullah"

It's normal to find prayer challenging at first. Consider using a prayer guide or app, and remember that improvement comes with practice. Learning from another Muslim in person can be extremely helpful.

Prayer Times

  • Fajr: Dawn, before sunrise
  • Dhuhr: Midday, after the sun passes its zenith
  • Asr: Afternoon
  • Maghrib: Just after sunset
  • Isha: Night, after twilight disappears

Prayer times vary by location and season. Use a prayer time app, mosque schedule, or website to find accurate times for your area.

Tips for New Muslims

  • Start with learning one prayer at a time
  • Use transliteration until you memorize the Arabic
  • Download a prayer app with step-by-step guidance
  • Practice with another Muslim if possible
  • Be patient with yourself as you learn
  • Focus on the meaning of what you're saying

Essential Lifestyle Changes

Halal Food

Muslims follow a dietary code called halal (permissible). This includes:

  • Avoiding pork and pork-derived products
  • Eating only meat that has been slaughtered according to Islamic guidelines
  • Avoiding alcohol and intoxicants
  • Avoiding food containing animal-derived ingredients from non-halal sources

Look for "halal certified" labels or eat vegetarian/seafood when unsure.

Islamic Dress

Islamic clothing emphasizes modesty:

  • For women: Covering the body except for face and hands, with loose-fitting clothes that don't reveal body shape
  • For men: Covering from navel to knee at minimum, with modest clothing

Many women choose to wear the hijab (headscarf) as part of their religious practice. Adopt Islamic dress at a pace that feels comfortable to you while respecting the principles.

Social Interactions

Islam encourages respectful interactions:

  • Maintaining modesty in mixed-gender settings
  • Avoiding physical contact with non-mahram (eligible for marriage) individuals of the opposite gender
  • Being mindful about attending events with alcohol or inappropriate activities

Finding balance in maintaining previous relationships while adhering to Islamic principles can be challenging but rewarding.

Character Development

Islam emphasizes developing good character:

  • Truthfulness and honesty
  • Patience and perseverance
  • Kindness and compassion
  • Humility and gratitude
  • Controlling anger and forgiving others

Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "I was sent to perfect good character." Working on character development is an essential part of being a Muslim.

Finding Community Support

Building connections with other Muslims is crucial for your journey:

Connecting with a Mosque

Visiting your local mosque can provide spiritual growth and community:

  • Attend Friday prayers (Jumu'ah)
  • Participate in educational programs
  • Join community events
  • Seek guidance from the imam

Most mosques welcome newcomers and have resources specifically for new Muslims.

Finding a Mentor

A knowledgeable mentor can provide personal guidance:

  • Ask at your local mosque for mentorship programs
  • Connect with revert support organizations
  • Look for community members who demonstrate good character
  • Consider online mentorship if local options are limited

Choose a mentor who is patient, knowledgeable, and respects your journey.

Navigating the Muslim Community

The Muslim community is diverse with various cultural practices:

  • Distinguish between religious requirements and cultural practices
  • Seek knowledge from reliable sources
  • Be patient with different perspectives
  • Focus on core principles rather than differences

Remember that Muslims are human and may not always represent Islam perfectly. Focus on the teachings rather than individuals' actions.

Family Relations

Navigating relationships with non-Muslim family can be challenging:

  • Maintain good relations with family members
  • Demonstrate Islam through good character
  • Be patient with misunderstandings
  • Share information gradually without overwhelming
  • Respect differing beliefs while maintaining your own

Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) emphasized maintaining family ties, even with family members of different faiths.

Continuing Your Islamic Education

Learning is a lifelong journey in Islam. Here are some key areas to focus on:

Quran Study

The Quran is Allah's direct word and the primary source of Islamic teachings:

  • Start with an accessible translation
  • Learn to recite in Arabic gradually
  • Join a Quran study circle
  • Use apps with recitation and translation

Even reading a small portion daily can have a profound impact on your spiritual growth.

Prophet's Life (Seerah)

Learning about Prophet Muhammad's life provides practical examples of Islamic teachings:

  • Read beginner-friendly biographies
  • Learn about his character and interactions
  • Study how he implemented Islamic teachings
  • Understand the historical context of Islam's development

The Prophet's life serves as the best model for implementing Islamic principles.

Arabic Language

Learning Arabic enhances your connection with Islamic texts:

  • Start with the Arabic alphabet
  • Learn common Islamic phrases
  • Study Quranic vocabulary
  • Consider online courses or local classes

Even basic Arabic knowledge can significantly enhance your understanding of prayers and the Quran.

Islamic Ethics and Laws

Understanding Islamic guidance for daily life situations:

  • Learn the principles behind Islamic rulings
  • Study fiqh (Islamic jurisprudence) basics
  • Understand differences of opinion on various matters
  • Apply ethical teachings in modern contexts

Focus on understanding the wisdom behind Islamic guidance rather than just rules.

Common Challenges and Solutions

Many new Muslims face similar challenges. Here are some common ones with potential solutions:

Difficulty with Prayer

Many new Muslims struggle with prayer routines:

  • Start with one or two prayers daily and gradually increase
  • Use prayer apps with notifications
  • Prepare prayer cheat sheets until memorized
  • Find a prayer partner for accountability

Remember that consistency is more important than perfection.

Family Opposition

Family resistance to conversion is common:

  • Demonstrate improved character rather than preaching
  • Maintain relationships and fulfill family duties
  • Seek compromise on cultural practices where possible
  • Find support from other Muslims facing similar situations

Time, patience, and good conduct often soften initial opposition.

Isolation and Loneliness

Feeling disconnected is a common experience:

  • Join activities at your local mosque
  • Connect with revert support groups
  • Maintain appropriate relations with previous friends
  • Volunteer for Islamic organizations
  • Consider online Muslim communities

Building a new social circle takes time—be patient and proactive.

Information Overload

Feeling overwhelmed by new information:

  • Focus on essentials first (beliefs and basic practices)
  • Take a structured approach to learning
  • Set realistic goals for implementing changes
  • Distinguish between obligations and recommendations
  • Remember that Islam is meant to be implemented gradually

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) established Islam over 23 years, not overnight.
